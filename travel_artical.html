Travel Article

By Mariela Perez University of Belize student and Toledo Campus Student Government Vice President

In the heart of Toledo, Belize, our journey unfolds amidst the delicate flutter of butterflies â€“ nature's vibrant storytellers. Imagine this: A scenic drive from Punta Gorda Town leads you to the hidden village of San Pedro Columbia, where the air is alive with the mesmerizing dance of these winged wonders. As John Keats once mused, 'A thing of beauty is a joy forever,' and precisely this joy awaits families from other parts of the world seeking adventure surrounded by beauty in the enchanting Butterfly Ecopark. It is a hidden gem accessible via local buses, just off the Southern Highway and approximately 1.4 miles from Lubantun. This tranquil haven is surrounded by nature and is a mere 15-minute walk from Lubantun.
As my friend Jenny and I neared our destination, I caught sight of a vast butterfly painting adorning the entrance door from a distance. Upon arrival, we ventured inside and strolled along a path lined with lush palm trees, accompanied by the distant melody of bird chirps. As we delved further into the surroundings, we were graciously greeted by Ms. Kile, the proprietor. While she endeavored to recollect my friend as one of her former students, my attention wandered to the right, where a pasture stretched out, inhabited by contented horses grazing on the verdant grass in the distance. Ms. Kile assured us that she would summon them with bananas once our tour concluded, a revelation that intrigued me with curiosity. Subsequently, she led us to the butterfly sanctuary, a quaint, well-crafted structure divided into two sections. One side resembled a serene garden, abounding with vibrant flowers where butterflies flitted gracefully among the blooms and cozy seating areas. The other housed separate enclosures for the Caligo Owl Wing and Blue morpho butterflies. Upon entry, we were greeted by the delightful aroma of citrus leaves and ripe bananas, enticing the graceful Caligo Owl Wing butterflies perched on a cement table. Their intricate circular patterns and vibrant hues captivated us as Mr. Marlon, our guide, enlightened us about their habits and life cycle. Transitioning to the Blue morpho butterflies, known for their vivid colors and friendly nature, we were enchanted by their fluttering presence, some even alighting gently on our hands as we admired their beauty. Mr. Marlon shared that these butterflies spend two weeks as caterpillars, feeding on banana leaves and other foliage cultivated in Ms. Kile's garden.
